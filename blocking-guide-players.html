Complete Coaching Guide · 7th–9th Grade
TEACHING BLOCKING FROM ZERO TO GAME-READY

Blocking is the most technically complex skill in volleyball — and the most undertaught at the middle school level. This guide builds your players from the ground up: hand position, footwork patterns, reading cues, team blocking, and progressive drills that actually work in a 60-minute practice.

BLOCK
Chapter 1
Why Blocking
Changes Everything
A well-timed block doesn't just stop a kill — it controls the entire opponent's offense, forces hitters into uncomfortable shots, and energizes your bench. At 7th–9th grade, even basic blocking fundamentals create a measurable competitive advantage.
⚡ The Real Value of Blocking
A stuff block scores a point directly. But even a touch block — redirecting the ball slightly — dramatically boosts your defense. A deflected ball is 3x easier to dig than a full-speed hit. Teach your players: every block attempt that influences the ball is a win. Stuffs are the bonus, not the goal.
The 3 Blocking Outcomes
✋
Stuff Block
Ball is stopped completely and lands on the opponent's side. Direct point. Creates massive momentum shift and intimidates hitters for the rest of the set.
↗️
Touch Block
Hands contact the ball and redirect it — slowing it down, changing direction, or popping it up. Makes the ball dramatically easier for your back row to dig. Just as valuable as a stuff over time.
📐
Channeling Block
Blocker deliberately seals one angle — forcing the hitter to hit a specific direction where your defense is positioned. Takes away the cross and leaves the line, or vice versa. Advanced, but teachable at 8th–9th grade.
The 6 Pillars of a Great Block
🙌
Hand Position & Penetration
Hands must cross the plane of the net — fingers spread wide, wrists firm, thumbs pointing up. A block that doesn't penetrate gives the hitter a free angle to swing through. Over the net, not into it.
⏰
Timing the Jump
Jump after the hitter leaves the ground — not at the same time. A blocker who jumps with the hitter will be coming down when the ball arrives. Watch the hitter's hip, not the ball.
🦶
Footwork to the Ball
Getting to the right position before the ball arrives. Three footwork patterns based on distance: step-close (short), crossover (medium), and run-step (long). Each has a specific technique.
👁️
Reading the Hitter
Shoulder angle, approach direction, arm swing path — all give away the shot before it happens. Reading is the difference between getting to the ball and guessing.
🤝
Team Blocking
Outside and middle blocker closing the gap between them — sealing out any space a hitter could thread. Communication and timing between two blockers must be practiced deliberately.
💬
Communication
"Line!" "Cross!" "Mine!" — blockers must call out loud where they think the set is going and who is taking the ball. Silent blocking is slow blocking.
Chapter 2
Blocking Technique
Step by Step
Before a player can block in a real play, they must own the mechanics in isolation. Build these 6 elements before adding movement, teammates, or live hitters.
1
Foundation
Ready Position at the Net
The starting point of every block attempt
▼

Before any movement happens, players need a consistent ready position. This is the platform from which all blocking begins. A bad ready position means slow first steps and compromised jumps.

  • 1Stand approximately one arm's length from the net — close enough to penetrate, far enough to not touch it
  • 2Feet shoulder-width apart, toes pointed forward (not out)
  • 3Knees slightly bent — loaded and ready to explode. Not standing straight.
  • 4Hands up at shoulder height, in front of the body — never at sides
  • 5Eyes on the setter, not the ball — tracking the offense, not the serve
Coaching Cues
  • Hands up before the set — always loaded
  • Bend your knees — if I can see your thighs you are too low, if you're standing straight you're too high
  • Eyes on the setter the whole time
Ready Position — Front View
2
Critical Skill
Hand Position & Penetration
The most impactful technical detail in blocking
▼

Bad hand position is the single biggest reason blocks don't work. Hands that stay on your own side of the net cannot stop anything. Every technical detail here is non-negotiable.

  • 1Spread fingers wide — as wide as physically possible. More surface area = more chances to touch the ball
  • 2Firm wrists, rigid arms — wrists that bend on contact deflect the ball back to your own side
  • 3Thumbs pointing UP — not together, not down. Up. This angles the hands forward over the net
  • 4Push hands OVER the net — penetrate into the opponent's side. Don't stop at the net tape
  • 5Angle hands DOWN toward the opponent's court — not straight up, not back toward your own side
  • 6Keep hands shoulder-width apart — a gap between your hands is a hole the hitter will find
Coaching Cues
  • Spread your fingers like you're catching a watermelon
  • Thumbs pointing to the ceiling before you jump
  • Push through the net — pretend there is a ball on the other side and you are trying to reach it
  • Lock your wrists — they do not move on contact
  • No gap between your hands — seal the window
🚨 Most Common Hand Error
Players put their hands at the net but don't penetrate. They're effectively making a wall on their own side — the ball hits their hands and deflects backward onto their own court. Penetration is not optional. It's the entire point of the block.
The Hand Check Drill
Player stands at the net, raises hands into block position, and freezes. Coach checks: fingers spread? Wrists firm? Thumbs up? Hands penetrating over net? Run this 30-second check at the start of every blocking session — takes 2 minutes for the whole team and fixes mechanical issues before they become habits.
3
Most Misunderstood
Jump Timing
Jump after the hitter — not with them
▼

The instinct is to jump when the hitter jumps. This is wrong. A blocker who jumps with the hitter will be at the peak of their jump — and coming down — when the ball is contacted. By the time the ball arrives at the net, the blocker's hands are below the net. Wait for the hitter's hip to rise above the net before leaving the ground.

The Timing Sequence
WRONG
Jump when hitter jumps → blocker already descending when ball is hit → hands below net on contact
BETTER
Jump when hitter's arm swings → slightly late, but at least at peak when ball is hit
CORRECT
Jump when hitter's hip clears the net height → blocker peaks at contact → maximum penetration
  • 1Watch the hitter's hip — not the ball, not the hands
  • 2When hitter's hip rises to net height → begin your jump
  • 3Drive arms upward from below waist — arm swing adds 2–4 inches
  • 4At peak: push hands forward and down — do not pull them back
Coaching Cues
  • Watch the hip, jump after the hip
  • Count it — ONE (hitter jumps)... TWO (you jump)
  • You should be going UP as they are swinging THROUGH
  • Never pull your hands back — only forward
  • If you're coming down before the hit, you jumped too early
🔧 Teaching Tip
Exaggerate the delay in practice. Make players count out loud "ONE" when the hitter leaves the ground, then jump on "TWO." It feels wrong at first — they'll be convinced they're jumping too late — but the contact timing will be perfect. After 2 weeks it becomes natural.
4
Safety & Transition
Safe Landing & Immediate Reset
Landing correctly prevents injury and enables transition
▼

How a blocker lands is as important as how they jump. A stiff landing risks ankle and knee injuries. A soft landing immediately positions the player for transition. Every blocking session must include landing mechanics — especially with 7th graders whose tendons are still developing.

  • 1Land on both feet simultaneously — not one foot first
  • 2Land on the balls of the feet — never flat-footed or heel-first
  • 3Bend knees immediately on landing to absorb force — soft and quiet
  • 4Do NOT reach back into the net while landing — hands clear of net
  • 5Immediately execute the drop step to begin transition (see Footwork chapter)
Coaching Cues
  • Land like a cat — silent feet, bent knees
  • Both feet at the same time, every time
  • Land and DROP — one motion, no pause
  • If your landing makes a loud noise, your knees are not bending
⚠️ Injury Warning
Landing with straight knees or landing on one foot are the two leading causes of knee and ankle injuries in youth volleyball. Run landing mechanics as a dedicated 3-minute drill at the start of every blocking session for the first 4 weeks. Make it non-negotiable before any jump blocking occurs.
Chapter 3
Blocking Footwork
Patterns
Three footwork patterns cover every blocking situation. Which one you use depends entirely on how far the blocker needs to travel. Teach Pattern 1 first — it covers 70% of all blocking situations at this level.
Rule of thumb: Footwork is not about speed — it's about arriving in balance. A blocker who sprints to position but is off-balance produces a worse block than a blocker who moves efficiently and arrives square. Teach balance first, then increase speed.
Pattern 1 — Step Close (Short Distance)
1
READ
Watch setter → where is the set going?
→
2
STEP
Lead foot steps toward the hitter
→
3
CLOSE
Trailing foot closes tight to lead foot
→
4
JUMP
Explode up off both feet simultaneously
→
5
LAND
Two feet, bent knees, then drop step
When to use: Moving 1–4 feet laterally — outside blocker closing on a set near their position, or a middle blocker reacting to a set that's close to center. The step-close keeps the body square so both hands stay even. This is the pattern to drill most.
Pattern 2 — Crossover Step (Medium Distance)
1
PIVOT
Turn lead foot to face direction of travel
→
2
CROSS
Dominant foot crosses in front of body
→
3
SQUARE
Plant foot squares hips back to net
→
4
JUMP
Two-foot takeoff from square position
→
5
LAND
Absorb and transition immediately
When to use: Moving 4–8 feet — middle blocker traveling to outside positions, outside blocker helping on a back set. The key is squaring up before jumping. A blocker who jumps while still turning will be twisted in the air — only one hand will be effective.
Pattern 3 — Step-Hop (Long Distance / Advanced)
1
PREP STEP
Small step to load lateral movement
→
2
CROSSOVER
Big crossing step to cover distance fast
→
3
HOP
Small hop-step to square up and load
→
4
JUMP
Explosive vertical from loaded hop position
→
5
LAND
Two feet, then transition drop step
When to use: Moving more than 8 feet — a middle blocker chasing a pushed set all the way to the pin, or covering for a partner blocker. The hop step is what separates this from a run — it reloads the legs for an explosive vertical jump. Teach only after Pattern 1 and 2 are solid.
Footwork Practice Progression
🚶
Week 1–2: Walk It
Walk through each footwork pattern in slow motion. No jumping. Coach watches every foot placement. Say the step name out loud as you do it: "step — close — jump." Repetition before speed.
🏃
Week 3: Run It
Same patterns at full speed. Add a simulated jump at the end — no ball. Coach calls "left" or "right" randomly, player executes the correct pattern. Time from cue to peak of jump.
🏐
Week 4+: Live It
Footwork in real blocking situations against live attackers. Coach identifies which pattern each player is using — correct any player who defaults to a shuffle when a crossover is needed.
Chapter 4
Reading the Hitter
Reading is the highest-leverage blocking skill — and the most neglected. A blocker who reads correctly can be in position before the ball leaves the hitter's hand. A blocker who reacts late is always scrambling, no matter how fast their feet are.
The Eyework Sequence: Setter → Ball → Hitter Approach → Hitter Shoulder → Hitter Elbow → Contact. Blockers must track this exact sequence on every rally. Missing any step means guessing at the wrong moment.
What to Read — In Order
1
Read First
Watch the Setter's Hands
The earliest possible read — tells you where the set is going before the hitter even starts their approach
▼

The setter's hand release angle tells you almost everything. Hands angling left = set to zone 4. Hands angling right = set to zone 2. Hands forward = back set. An experienced blocker is already beginning their footwork while the ball is still in the setter's hands.

  • →Hands angling toward zone 4 (left front) = outside set left
  • →Hands angling toward zone 2 (right front) = right side set
  • →Setter facing away from usual direction = back set incoming
  • →Very quick, low release = quick set or shoot set to the middle
Coaching Cues
  • Eyes on the setter from the first pass — never look away
  • Your feet should react to the setter's hands — not the ball
  • Call it out loud: "Outside!" "Right side!" the moment you read it
  • Your teammates need to hear what you see — call it every time
2
Read Second
Watch the Hitter's Approach Angle
Approach direction predicts shot direction — 80% of the time
▼

Most hitters approach in the same direction they intend to hit. An approach cutting inside usually means a cross-court hit. An approach coming straight means a line shot is likely. Watch the approach direction as a strong secondary clue.

  • →Approach cutting sharply inward (toward middle) → cross-court hit likely
  • →Approach staying outside, parallel to sideline → line shot likely
  • →Very fast approach → hitter is timing an aggressive swing, rarely tips
  • →Slow floating approach → tip or roll highly likely, jump later
Coaching Cues
  • Watch their feet — where are they coming from?
  • A hitter cutting inside is setting up the cross — shift your hands inside
  • A hitter floating in slow = probably a tip — stay patient, don't jump early
3
Read Third — Final Cue
Watch the Hitter's Shoulder
The last read before jumping — the most reliable shot predictor
▼

The hitting shoulder's angle at the top of the jump is the single best predictor of shot direction. An open shoulder (facing cross-court) means a cross-court swing. A closed shoulder (facing the line) means a line shot. Elite blockers read this automatically.

  • →Open shoulder (perpendicular to sideline, chest facing cross-court) = cross-court attack
  • →Closed shoulder (parallel to sideline, chest facing the line) = line attack
  • →Elbow high and behind head = hard powerful swing incoming — block aggressively
  • →Elbow low or bent = tip or roll incoming — hands forward, don't jump high
Coaching Cues
  • Open chest = cross. Closed chest = line. Simple.
  • High elbow = big swing. Low elbow = tip. React accordingly.
  • Make a call every single time — even wrong calls train the read habit
  • Don't look at the ball — look at the shoulder
Teaching the Shoulder Read
Run the Shoulder Read Drill from the Transition guide: player A simulates a hitting motion, player B calls "line" or "cross" based on shoulder — before contact. No ball needed. This drill builds the read reflex faster than any live blocking drill. Run it 15 reps per player every session for 4 weeks.
Chapter 5
Team Blocking
One blocker can slow a hitter. Two blockers working together can eliminate half the court. Team blocking requires timing, communication, and closing the seam between blockers. This is where individual skill becomes a system.
The 3 Responsibilities
📋
Outside Blocker Sets the Block
The outside blocker (on the side where the set is going) establishes position first. She does NOT move until she reads the set. She jumps and penetrates — her position determines where the seam is. The middle blocker must close to her.
🏃
Middle Blocker Closes the Seam
The middle blocker moves laterally to join the outside blocker. Her inside hand must touch — or nearly touch — the outside blocker's inside hand. Any gap between them is a hole the hitter will find. She jumps slightly after the outside blocker.
💬
Communication Throughout
"Outside!" when the set is tracked. "Go!" when the outside blocker commits. "Closed!" when the seam is sealed. Verbal cues between blockers reduce hesitation and prevent both players from moving the same direction on a fake set.
Closing the Seam — Step by Step
The Seam Closing Sequence
  • 1Set is tracked by both blockers. Both call out direction: "Outside! Outside!"
  • 2Outside blocker moves to the hitter's hitting shoulder and sets her position at the net
  • 3Middle blocker closes quickly — her outside foot plants next to outside blocker's inside foot
  • 4Both blockers load — outside blocker jumps first, middle blocker jumps 0.2 seconds later
  • 5In the air: outside blocker's inside hand and middle blocker's outside hand close the seam
  • 6Both penetrate over the net together — one unified wall, not two separate blocks
Sealing Line vs Cross
Seal the Line
Outside blocker positions hand on the outside (line side) of the ball — takes away the line shot. Back row defender at zone 5 (line) shifts to cover cross-court. Middle blocker closes seam. Hitter is forced to hit cross into the waiting back row defense.
Seal the Cross
Outside blocker shifts hands inside — takes away the cross-court angle. Back row at zone 1 (cross-court) shifts to cover the open line. Works best when your outside blocker reads the hitter's shoulder showing open = cross.
Swing Blocking
Advanced: blocker swings hands from outside to inside (or vice versa) mid-air to take away a shot the hitter reveals late. Teach only after the basic technique is mastered — requires reading the shoulder in the air.
Team Block — Seam Closed (Top View)
OB and MB seal the seam directly in front of the attacker. Back row defenders cover unblocked zones.
Chapter 7
8 Common Blocking
Mistakes
These are the errors you will see every single practice. Recognizing them instantly — and knowing the fix — separates a coach who speeds up improvement from one who watches the same mistake for 3 months.
🧱
Hands Not Penetrating
WRONG Hands stop at the net tape — no penetration over to the opponent's side. Ball hits hands and deflects back into your own court.
FIX Run the Hand Check Drill every session. Cue: "Reach for the ball on THEIR side — not the tape." Make players touch the top of the net with their wrists, then push forward.
⏰
Jumping Too Early
WRONG Blocker jumps when the hitter jumps — already at peak and descending when contact happens. Hands are below the net when the ball arrives.
FIX Teach the "ONE-TWO" count. Hitter leaves ground = ONE. Blocker jumps = TWO. Exaggerate the delay in practice until it feels natural. Takes 3–4 weeks to retrain this instinct.
🫳
Bent Wrists
WRONG Wrists flex back on contact — turning the hands into a ramp that deflects the ball upward or backward instead of downward into the opponent's court.
FIX Cue: "Wrists are concrete — they do not move." Have players push their hands against a wall pre-practice, feeling the locked wrist position. 1 week of daily hand checks eliminates this.
🤸
Jumping Away From Net
WRONG Blocker jumps backward to avoid touching the net — resulting in zero penetration and a block that can never reach the ball.
FIX Cue: "Jump UP, not backward." Mark a line on the floor. Feet must stay on the line through takeoff. If they jump backward, feet will visibly leave the line.
🕳️
Gap Between Hands
WRONG Space between the two blockers' hands — or between one blocker's hands — gives the hitter a hole to thread. Elite hitters find this gap every time.
FIX In solo blocking: thumbs must nearly touch. In team blocking: inside hands must close. Run seam closing drill and physically require blockers to touch hands at the seam before considering the rep clean.
👀
Watching the Ball, Not the Hitter
WRONG Blocker watches the set travel to the hitter instead of watching the setter then immediately shifting to the hitter's shoulder. Leads to late positioning every time.
FIX Run the Eye Progression Drill. Enforce: "Eyes from setter to shoulder — skip the ball in the air." Call out any player watching the ball instead of the hitter. Takes 2–3 weeks of consistent correction.
🤐
Silence at the Net
WRONG Blockers do not call the set direction, do not communicate who is taking the ball, and don't call their jump. The back row is flying blind and cannot adjust to the block's angle.
FIX Make verbal communication mandatory — deduct a rep if there is silence. Cue: "If I can't hear you from the sideline, your block doesn't count." Takes 4 weeks of consistent enforcement to become automatic.
🛑
Stiff Landing
WRONG Landing with locked knees — creates a loud "thud," risks ankle and knee injury, and prevents any transition movement. The player is planted and unable to drop step.
FIX Cue: "Land like a cat — silent feet." Run landing mechanics for 3 minutes at the start of every blocking session for the first 4 weeks. Loud landings get an immediate verbal correction.
