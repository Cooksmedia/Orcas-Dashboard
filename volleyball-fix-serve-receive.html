Problem Solver Drill Plan

FIX THE
WEAK SPOTS

Targeted drills and progressions to solve serve receive footwork and fear of the ball — two of the most common problems at the 7th–9th grade level.

🦶 Footwork & Positioning 😰 Ball Fear Desensitization 📈 Progressive Difficulty 60 Min Sessions
🦶
Problem 1 — Footwork
Not Moving to the Ball. Waiting Instead of Reading.
Players are reacting to where the ball already is instead of reading where it's going. Feet are set late, body angle is wrong, and they're making contact off-platform — or missing entirely.
⚠️ Root Cause: Players are watching the ball flight, not the server's contact point. They don't know how to read where the ball will go before it leaves the server's hand.
😰
Problem 2 — Ball Fear
Flinching, Backing Up, Letting Balls Drop.
When a hard or fast ball comes at them — especially high and toward the face — players flinch, bail out, or raise their arms defensively instead of holding platform and moving forward to meet it.
⚠️ Root Cause: Their self-protection instinct overrides their training. The fix isn't "don't be scared" — it's progressive exposure that builds real trust between their face and a moving ball.
🦶 Footwork & Positioning Drills
01
Footwork Drill
Read the Server's Contact Point
⏱ 8 min 👥 Full Team 📍 Serve Receive Line
▼

The #1 fix for late footwork is teaching players to read before the ball is served. They should be moving to their spot while the ball is still in the air — not after it bounces or dips toward them.

How to Run It
  • 1Server stands at the service line. Receivers in W-formation (3 players).
  • 2BEFORE the serve, coach calls "FREEZE" — server holds toss. Receivers must call where they think it's going: "Mine left!" or "Short zone 5!"
  • 3Server releases. Did they call it right? Give feedback immediately.
  • 4Progress to calling it AND moving 2 steps before contact.
  • 5Run 15 reps per group, rotate servers.
Coaching Cues — Say These Out Loud
  • Watch the toss, not the server's face
  • Your feet move before the ball crosses the net
  • Call it before it gets to you
  • Two steps toward the ball, every time
  • Early is right. Late is wrong. Always.
Progression — Increase Difficulty Over 3 Weeks
WEEK 1Coach tosses underhand — predictable, slow
WEEK 2Teammate serves — real serves, shorter range
WEEK 3Random zone serves — no warning, game speed
02
Footwork Drill
Shuffle to the Ball — No Crossover Rule
⏱ 6 min 👥 Groups of 3 📍 Half Court
▼

Players who cross their feet when moving laterally lose their platform angle on contact. This drill forces the correct shuffle-step footwork pattern so they arrive at the ball with their body already facing the target.

How to Run It
  • 1Place 3 cones in a line, 6 feet apart. Player starts at middle cone.
  • 2Coach points left or right — player shuffle-steps (no crossover) to that cone, touches it with outside hand, shuffles back.
  • 3Add a ball: coach tosses to left or right side. Player must shuffle, not step-cross, to pass the ball back.
  • 4If player crosses feet = redo that rep. No exceptions.
  • 510 reps each player, then rotate.
Coaching Cues
  • Stay square to the target, even while moving
  • Shoulder points where you want the ball to go
  • Get there early, pause, then pass
  • Shuffle first, arms second — not at the same time
  • Low hips = faster feet. Stand up = dead feet.
Progression
STARTNo ball — just footwork with cones
NEXTAdd soft toss, pass to self
ADVANCEDAdd live serve, must shuffle-pass to target
03
Footwork Drill
Ball Drop Reaction — First Step Trigger
⏱ 5 min 👥 Pairs 📍 Anywhere
▼

This drill trains the first step — the most important step in serve receive. Most players' first step is too slow. This makes it fast and automatic.

How to Run It
  • 1Partner A holds a ball at shoulder height, arm extended to side. Player B in athletic stance, 6 feet away.
  • 2Partner A drops the ball without warning. Player B must catch it before second bounce.
  • 3Increase distance each round. Move to left AND right sides.
  • 4Add a rule: first step MUST be a shuffle (not a reach-step). Penalize reach-steps.
  • 5Progress to: partner tosses instead of drops — adds reading element.
Coaching Cues
  • Eyes on the ball, feet already loaded
  • First step is always toward the ball
  • Don't wait — go NOW
  • Your body should be moving before your brain says move
04
Footwork Drill
Platform Angle Gate Drill
⏱ 8 min 👥 Groups of 4 📍 Back Row
▼

Players often have the right footwork but the wrong body angle — so even a well-timed pass goes sideways. This drill teaches them to face their target FIRST, then platform second.

How to Run It
  • 1Set up two cones as a "gate" — 3 feet wide — representing the setter target at zone 3. Place it 20 feet from the passers.
  • 2Coach tosses from various angles. Player must pass through the gate.
  • 3Before passing, player must say "GATE" to confirm they see the target.
  • 4Track: how many of 10 passes go through the gate? Set a team goal (start at 5/10, build to 8/10).
  • 5Add live serves for advanced players.
Coaching Cues
  • Hips face the gate before the ball gets to you
  • You're not passing the ball — you're directing traffic
  • Point your belly button to zone 3
  • See the target. Move your hips. Then pass.
😰 Ball Fear Desensitization Drills
⚠️ Coach's Note: Run these drills in order. Do NOT skip to the harder ones for a player who hasn't mastered the earlier ones. The goal is to build real confidence — not to force it. Praise bravery loudly and publicly. One "she didn't flinch!" in front of the team does more than 10 reps alone.
F1
Ball Fear — Stage 1 (Lowest Pressure)
Self-Toss Face Level Hold
⏱ 4 min 👥 Solo 😊 No Fear Required
▼

Build trust between the ball and the face slowly. No one throws at anyone in this stage. The player is in full control.

How to Run It
  • 1Player holds ball at face height with both hands, arms extended — ball 12 inches from face.
  • 2Slowly move ball toward face and back, 10 times. Eyes stay open. Breathe.
  • 3Toss ball 1 foot above face and catch it. Repeat 10 times. Eyes OPEN.
  • 4Toss 2 feet above face. Let it bounce off forehead (gently). No dodging.
  • 5Partner holds ball 3 feet away, releases — player headers it back. No flinching goal.
What to Say to the Player
  • The ball cannot hurt you when YOU control it
  • Eyes open means you're in charge of the ball
  • Every rep tells your brain: I'm safe
  • We're not rushing. There's no wrong answer here.
F2
Ball Fear — Stage 2 (Building Trust)
Soft Toss to Platform — Eyes Forward Rule
⏱ 6 min 👥 Pairs 😐 Mild Challenge
▼

Now someone else is throwing the ball. It's aimed at them on purpose. The key rule: eyes never leave the ball. If they look away = reset.

How to Run It
  • 1Partner tosses UNDERHAND directly at player's platform from 8 feet away. SLOW. No pace.
  • 2Player passes back. Eyes never leave ball. If they look away = rep doesn't count.
  • 3After 5 clean reps, partner tosses slightly above platform toward chest height.
  • 4After 5 more clean reps, toss toward chin/shoulder height. Player holds platform up to meet it.
  • 5Celebrate: "YOU KEPT YOUR EYES OPEN!" This is the win, not the pass quality.
Coaching Cues
  • Track the ball from the second it leaves their hand
  • Step INTO the ball, not away from it
  • Your platform is a wall. The ball bounces off. It doesn't hurt.
  • You cannot get hurt by something you're watching
  • Forward is brave. Backward is giving up the point.
F3
Ball Fear — Stage 3 (Pace Introduction)
The Courage Wall — Absorb the Speed
⏱ 8 min 👥 Groups of 3 😤 Real Challenge
▼

Introduce pace in a controlled way. The player learns that a faster ball is actually easier to pass than a slow floater — because pace = more control off the platform.

How to Run It
  • 1Player stands 4 feet from the net, facing it. Coach is on other side hitting DOWN balls (no jump, just firm arm swing over net).
  • 2Ball is hit directly at the player's midsection — not fast, not slow. Medium pace.
  • 3Player's job: arms form platform, absorb the ball, redirect up. Do NOT back up.
  • 4After 5 reps at midsection — move balls to shoulder height. Same rule: hold platform.
  • 5Key teaching: "The faster the ball comes at you, the less YOU have to swing. Just hold still and let the ball do the work."
The Big Mindset Shift — Say This
  • A fast ball is your FRIEND. It bounces high off your platform.
  • You don't swing at it — you CATCH it with your arms
  • Move toward the ball. Forward feet = control.
  • If it hits you, so what? That's just a rep. Try again.
  • Brave means scared AND doing it anyway
F4
Ball Fear — Stage 4 (Game Speed)
Stand Your Ground — Live Attack Receive
⏱ 10 min 👥 Full Team 🔥 Game Speed
▼

Full game-speed attacks directed at players. This is where the desensitization pays off. Only run this after stages 1–3 are solid. Never start here.

How to Run It
  • 1Standard defensive positions. Attacker hits from position 4 (left side).
  • 2Coach tells hitter to aim at Zone 6 (middle back) — the most intimidating ball for newer defenders.
  • 3Defenders dig balls. Count consecutive digs as a team. Goal: 5 in a row.
  • 4Add rule: if anyone steps back or flinches = counter resets to 0. Team must hold ground.
  • 5Celebrate loudly when players hold ground even if they miss the dig. The brave read = the win.
Coaching Cues
  • Your feet stay planted until AFTER you read the ball
  • Move TOWARD — even half a step counts
  • A missed dig from a brave position is better than a made dig from a scared position
  • That's how your body learns. Rep by rep.
⚡ Combined Integration — Both Problems Together
★
Integration Drill — Use After 3 Weeks
Move, Hold, Pass — The Full Package Drill
⏱ 12 min 👥 Full Team 📍 Full Court Serve Receive
▼

This drill wraps footwork reading AND ball courage into one competitive game. It replicates exactly what happens in a match.

How to Run It
  • 13 receivers in W-formation. Server serves from full distance — mix of float and hard serves.
  • 2Scoring: 3 points = pass that hits the setter target. 2 points = pass stays on their side. 1 point = they moved toward the ball and touched it. 0 = they backed up or flinched.
  • 3Coach tracks the score out loud. Team competes against their own best.
  • 4After 10 serves, rotate receivers. Keep a leaderboard.
  • 5At the end: recognize the player who scored highest on MOVEMENT COURAGE — not just passing accuracy.
What You're Looking For
  • Feet moving before ball crosses the net
  • No backward steps on hard serves
  • Platform held steady on high balls
  • Calling the ball before it arrives
  • Square hips to zone 3 at contact

🧠 What to Say (and Not Say) to Scared Players
✅
DO Say This
"I saw you move toward that ball — that's huge." Praise the action, not just the result. A missed dig with brave feet is worth more than a made pass from a scared position.
❌
Don't Say This
"Stop being scared" or "just go get it!" These phrases shame the player without giving them tools. Fear is a physical response, not a choice. Work the drills, not the emotions.
🏆
Reward Bravery Publicly
When a player who normally flinches holds her platform on a hard ball — STOP PRACTICE and celebrate it. That one moment of public recognition can break the fear pattern faster than 50 reps.
🔄
Give Them a Reset Cue
Teach one physical cue they use when fear kicks in: "Two steps forward, arms out." A physical action interrupts the freeze response. Same cue, every time, until it's automatic.
👥
Pair Them Strategically
Put hesitant players beside your most fearless passer in serve receive. Confidence is contagious at this age. Watching a teammate charge the ball repeatedly reprograms what "normal" looks like.
📈
Track Their Progress Visibly
Keep a simple "courage board" — every time a player holds ground on a hard serve, they get a tally mark. It makes their growth visible and turns bravery into a thing they want to collect.
