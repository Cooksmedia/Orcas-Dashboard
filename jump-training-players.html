The Foundation
Why Players Don't Jump Higher

Most players think jumping is about leg strength alone. It's not. The vertical jump is a combination of 4 physical systems — and all 4 need to be trained. Ignore one and you leave inches on the floor.

⚡
Reactive Strength
The ability to absorb force and immediately redirect it upward. This is trained through plyometrics — box jumps, depth jumps, and bounding. Most undertrained system at this age level.
💪
Concentric Power
Raw muscle force production in the quads, glutes, and calves. Trained through squats, single-leg work, and calf raises. The engine behind every jump.
🎯
Approach Mechanics
How a player converts horizontal speed into vertical height. The 4-step approach, arm swing, and two-foot plant can add 2–4 inches instantly with no fitness change at all.
🔄
Hip Mobility & Flexibility
Tight hips and ankles limit how deep a player can load before jumping. Mobility work allows a deeper pre-jump dip — which means more stored energy to release upward.
The Rule
Train All 4 Systems. Every Week.

This program cycles through all four systems weekly so no gains are left on the table. Plyometrics 2x per week. Strength 2x per week. Technique every practice. Mobility every session as part of warm-up. That's the formula.

The Science of Progress
What Changes in Your Body
🧠
Neural Firing Rate
Weeks 1–4 gains come mostly from your nervous system learning to fire muscles faster and in better sequence — not muscle growth. This is why proper form from Day 1 matters so much.
🏋️
Muscle Fiber Recruitment
Weeks 4–8 your body starts recruiting more fast-twitch fibers. These are the fibers responsible for explosive power. Plyometrics are the fastest way to wake them up.
📈
Tendon Stiffness
Weeks 8–12 tendons become stiffer and more elastic, storing and releasing energy like springs. This is the "pop" elite athletes feel — and it takes time to develop safely.
⚠️
The Safety Rule
Never do max-intensity plyometrics two days in a row. Tendons need 48 hours to recover. Skipping rest days is the #1 reason young athletes get knee injuries from jump training.

How to Test Correctly
Standing Reach
Shoes off. Stand flat against a wall. Reach both arms up as high as possible. Mark or measure the highest point. This is your baseline.
Jump Reach
Stand 6 inches from wall. Jump from two feet (no step). Touch the wall at highest point. Best of 3 attempts counts.
Approach Jump
Same as above but use your full 4-step approach. This measures volleyball-specific vertical — usually 3–5" higher than standing jump.
Test Conditions
Always test after a full warm-up but before any hard training. Same time of day each test. Same shoes (or barefoot). Consistency = valid data.
For the Coach
Making It Work

Jump training with young athletes is powerful — and requires careful management. These principles separate programs that produce results from ones that cause injuries.

01
Land Before You Launch
Before any high-intensity plyometrics, athletes must be able to land perfectly — soft knees, quiet feet, hips back. If the landing is stiff or noisy, the tendons are not ready. Never skip the landing mechanics progression.
02
Quality Over Quantity
5 perfect box jumps are worth more than 20 sloppy ones. Stop a set the moment form breaks down. Fatigue + plyometrics = injury risk. Teach athletes to recognize when they're done for the day.
03
The 48-Hour Rule
Never schedule high-intensity plyometrics two days in a row. Tendons don't recover overnight. Space plyo sessions with at least one full rest or low-impact day between them. This is non-negotiable for athletes under 16.
04
Fix the Approach First
The biggest free gains come from approach mechanics — not fitness. A player with a perfect 4-step approach and proper arm swing can gain 3–5 inches instantly with zero strength change. Start here every season.
05
Celebrate Small Gains
A half-inch improvement is significant for a developing athlete. Track and celebrate every measurement. Seeing numbers go up is one of the most powerful motivators you can give a 7th–9th grader.
06
Work Both Legs Equally
Single-leg exercises (Bulgarian split squats, single-leg box jumps) are as important as bilateral work. Imbalances between legs are one of the leading causes of knee injuries in adolescent female athletes.
07
Warm-Up Is Not Optional
Cold muscles and tendons don't jump well and injure easily. Minimum 8-minute dynamic warm-up before any jump training: leg swings, hip circles, bodyweight squats, skipping, ankle rolls. Non-negotiable.
08
In-Season Maintenance
During the season, drop volume to 1x per week plyometrics and 1x strength. Don't stop entirely — gains disappear in 3 weeks without stimulus. Maintenance keeps what was built during the off-season.
The Fastest Way to Add Inches

In order of impact: 1) Fix approach arm swing — most players barely use their arms, costing 2–4 inches immediately. 2) Hip mobility — tight hips cap the pre-jump load. 10 minutes of hip stretching daily adds inches within 2 weeks. 3) Calf raises — the calf is the last muscle to fire in a jump and the most undertrained. 100 single-leg calf raises per day produces measurable results in 3–4 weeks.
