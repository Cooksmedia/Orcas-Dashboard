Chapter 1
Why Setting
Runs Everything

The setter is the quarterback of volleyball. Every offensive action — every attack, every combination play, every tempo adjustment — flows through the setter's hands. A team's offensive ceiling is determined almost entirely by setter quality.

2nd
The setter contacts the ball on the 2nd touch of every rally. That means the setter makes more decisions under pressure than any other player on the court — every single point.
The Setter's Job
The setter has three responsibilities on every 2nd touch: (1) deliver a hittable ball to the right location at the right height, (2) choose the right attacker based on pass quality and blocker position, and (3) disguise the decision as long as possible so blockers can't read it. All three happen in under one second.
Consistency Over Creativity
Young setters try to be creative too early. The first goal is consistency — a hittable ball to Zone 4, every time, from any pass. Once that's automatic, add the back set. Then the quick. Then seam decisions. Setters who try to run combinations before they can consistently set Zone 4 hurt their team more than they help.
Court Awareness
The setter must know where all 5 other players are before contacting the ball. This is a trained skill — run drills where the setter calls out blocker positions or attacker positions before setting. Court awareness is the difference between a setter who runs offense and a setter who just gets the ball over.
Leadership on the Court
The setter controls the tempo and energy of the offense. A setter who panics when passes are poor causes hitters to doubt the offense. A setter who is calm under any pass quality — finding the best option no matter what comes — builds offensive confidence across the whole team. Teach composure as explicitly as you teach hand shape.
Chapter 2
Hand Shape & Contact

Setting hand shape is the most technical skill in volleyball. The hands must form a consistent, symmetrical window above the forehead — and contact must be simultaneous on both hands. Any inconsistency produces a double contact call or an uncontrollable ball.

Key 1
The Triangle Window
Raise both hands above the forehead, thumbs and index fingers forming a triangle (or diamond) shape. The ball should be visible through this triangle before contact. Fingers spread naturally — not rigid, not floppy. The contact surface is the finger pads, not the palms.
Cues
"Make a triangle, look through it"
"Fingers spread like you're grabbing a ball"
"Hands up before the ball gets there"
Key 2
Simultaneous Contact
Both hands must contact the ball at exactly the same time. One hand touching before the other = double contact = illegal. This is the most whistled setting violation at the youth level. Drill slow-motion setting with a partner watching for any gap between hand contacts.
Cues
"Both hands hit the ball — not one then two"
"Clap test: hands arrive together"
"Symmetrical — same pressure both hands"
Key 3
Contact Point & Release
Contact happens above and slightly in front of the forehead — not behind the head, not at chest level. The release follows the target direction: extend arms and legs together through the ball toward the target. The finish should have both arms extended toward where the set is going.
Cues
"Contact above the hairline, not behind the head"
"Extend toward your target — finish tall"
"Legs push through the set — not just arms"
Key 4
Soft Hands vs. Hard Hands
Soft hands = fingers absorb the ball slightly at contact — the ball compresses briefly before being released. This is legal and produces consistent sets. Hard hands = stiff, rigid fingers that don't give — results in a harder, less controlled set and higher chance of a double contact call. Develop soft hands through slow-motion catching and setting drills.
Cues
"Catch and throw in one motion"
"Feel the ball load into your fingers"
"Relaxed hands, not stiff"
Key 5
Wrists at Finish
Wrists should snap slightly outward at the finish — not inward (which causes spin) or dropping down (which means late contact). The finish with wrists rotated slightly outward helps release the ball cleanly without adding side spin. Most young setters finish with palms facing each other — this creates inward spin on the ball.
Cues
"Thumbs out at the finish"
"Palms face the ceiling after you release"
"Wrists away, not toward each other"
Chapter 3
Setter Footwork

A setter who arrives at the setting position early — feet set, body facing Zone 4, hands ready — can set any ball that comes within reach. A setter who arrives late is always reacting, always adjusting, and always giving hitters a less hittable set.

The Setting Zone
The setter's target position is at the net between Zones 2 and 3, facing Zone 4. The setter should reach this position before the ball does. In a 6-2, the setter penetrates from the back row on the serve — they must move immediately and arrive early. Early arrival = time to read the pass and choose the set. Late arrival = desperation.
The Right Foot Rule
A right-handed setter's right foot should be slightly in front at contact — this opens the body to Zone 4 for the primary (front) set while allowing the back set without a turn. The right foot forward position makes both directions available without telegraphing which way the ball is going.
3 Footwork Approaches
  • Square Step: Ball coming directly to the setter's position. Simple plant and set — no adjustment needed. Both feet square to Zone 4. Used for perfect passes.
  • Side Step (shuffle): Ball 2–4 feet off target. Shuffle to get under it while maintaining the right foot forward position. The most common footwork adjustment in a match.
  • Crossover Step: Ball significantly away from the setting position (tight pass, shank, or off-speed serve). Run to the ball, plant right foot first, and set from wherever you are. This is emergency footwork — drill it so setters don't panic when the pass isn't perfect.
Eyes on the Pass First
Setters must watch the pass all the way to their hands before shifting attention to hitters and blockers. Young setters look at hitters too early and take their eyes off the ball — causing late contact and uncontrolled sets. Ball first, hitters second.
Penetration in 6-2
In a 6-2, the setter runs from the back row after the serve. She must start moving the moment the server makes contact — not when the ball crosses the net. Any delay means arriving late. Drill the penetration timing explicitly: server contacts ball → setter releases and runs → setter arrives before pass.
Chapter 4
Tempo & Set Types

Different sets have different heights, locations, and timing — and together they create a varied offense that blockers struggle to read. For youth teams, start with two sets and add a third only when the first two are consistent.

Start Here: Two Sets
Before teaching tempo or combination plays, every setter needs two reliable sets: (1) a high outside set to Zone 4 (the "Go" or "Hut") that any hitter can approach and attack in system or out of system, and (2) a high back set to Zone 2 (right side). Once these two are consistent from any pass location, introduce a third set — typically a middle quick or a Zone 4 at tempo.
High Outside Set (Zone 4)
The foundation of every youth offense. 3–4 feet off the net, 8–10 feet high, landing near the Zone 4 antenna. The height gives the hitter time to approach and the set landing near the antenna gives them a clean angle. The most common error: setting too tight to the net (hitter hits the tape) or too far off the net (hitter swings into a block).
Back Set (Zone 2)
Set directly behind the setter to the right side hitter. Same height as the outside set — new setters often set the back set lower, making it harder to hit. The key: the wrists finish pointing left (toward Zone 4) to disguise the back set as long as possible. The hitter should be in approach before the set leaves the setter's hands.
Quick Set (Middle)
A fast, lower set to the middle blocker running in front of the setter. Contact happens just before or as the hitter jumps — not after. Height: 1–2 feet above the net tape. Too high and blockers have time to close; too low and it's impossible to hit. Introduce this only when the outside and back sets are automatic.
Setter Dump
On the 2nd contact, the front-row setter attacks the ball over the net themselves — usually a 1-handed tip to an open spot. Best used when: Zone 3 or Zone 6 on the opponent's side is uncovered, the blockers are focused on hitters, or to slow down a run. Teach the dump only after front sets are reliable.
Chapter 5
Setting Decisions

Reading and decision-making separate average setters from great setters. These aren't natural gifts — they're trained skills. Here's how to systematically develop a setter who makes smart decisions every rally, not just good swings.

The Decision Framework
Teach setters to read two things before every set: (1) Pass quality — how good was the pass? Perfect pass opens all options. Poor pass forces a high ball. (2) Blocker position — where is the middle blocker going? Where is the right-side blocker? The best set is the one that gives a hitter the best chance — not always the hitter's favorite set.
Reading Blockers
Before contacting the ball, a setter's peripheral vision should catch the middle blocker's starting position and direction of movement. Middle cheating outside? Set middle. Middle flat-footed? Set outside. Over time, setters develop the ability to read without looking — but it must be drilled explicitly before it becomes instinct.
Disguise
The longer the setter waits to reveal their decision, the less time the blockers have to react. Teach setters to face Zone 4 regardless of where the set is going, keep hands symmetrical through the loading phase, and commit to the set direction only at the last moment. A setter whose hands "telegraph" the direction early gives the middle blocker 0.3 seconds more time to move.
Out-of-System Decisions
When the pass is poor — too tight, too far off the net, too short — the setter's only job is to get the ball to the best available hitter at a hittable height. In out-of-system situations: set high to the outside, don't try to be clever, give the hitter time to adjust. The best out-of-system set is a high ball to Zone 4, every time.
Building Decision Habits in Practice
Run "read drills" where the setter must call out the block position before setting: "Middle closing left — setting right!" Coaches hold up fingers (1, 2, or 3) indicating which set to run — setters must read, call, and set within 1 second. Track correct decisions as a percentage. Setters who make the right call 70%+ of the time in training will execute well in matches.
Chapter 6 · Coach Only
Setting Drills

These drills develop hand shape, footwork, consistency, and decision-making. Setters need daily repetitions — even 10 minutes of toss-and-set solo work before practice produces significant improvement over a season.

Solo · Hand Shape
Ceiling Sets
1 player5 minNo court needed

Setup: Player lies on their back with one ball. Set the ball straight up to the ceiling and catch it.

Drill: Set the ball vertically above the face, aiming for consistent height (mark a target height with tape on the ceiling if possible). 3 sets of 20. The ball should return to the same contact point every time.

Coaching Focus: Hand shape. Lying on the back removes all footwork variables and focuses entirely on hand position, simultaneous contact, and consistent release. The best drill for fixing double-contact issues.

Solo · Footwork
Wall Set — Footwork Approach
1 player8 minWall

Setup: Player stands 5–8 feet from a wall. Set the ball off the wall and catch the rebound, re-set, repeat.

Drill: Set the ball to a spot on the wall, move feet to get behind the rebound, and set again without letting the ball drop. Focus on right foot forward position on every contact. 3 minutes continuous.

Coaching Focus: Does the setter move their feet before extending arms? Setters who reach rather than step are developing bad habits. Stop, reset, emphasize feet first.

Partner · Consistency
Target Zone Setting
2 players10 minFull court

Setup: Setter at Zone 3 position, partner at Zone 4 (left front). Cone or hoop marking the target landing spot.

Drill: Partner tosses to setter from Zone 1 or 6. Setter sets to Zone 4, aiming to land within 3 feet of the cone. 15 reps, track percentage landing in target area. Switch to back-set Zone 2 for second half.

Coaching Focus: Consistency, not height. A set that lands in the same spot every time gives hitters confidence to approach before the set arrives. Track improvement week over week.

Decision Making
Read & Call Drill
4–6 players12 minFull court

Setup: Setter, 3 blockers on opposite side, 2 hitters. Passer tosses to setter.

Drill: Setter must call out "Front!" or "Back!" or "Middle!" before setting, based on blocker positioning. Coach awards 2 points for correct call + good set, 1 point for good set with wrong call, 0 for error. 15 reps.

Coaching Focus: Is the setter reading blockers before the ball arrives or after? After is too late. Setters should scan blockers during the pass trajectory, not after they catch the ball.

Out of System
Bad Pass Setting
3 players8 minFull court

Setup: Passer intentionally passes badly (too tight, too far off net, too short). Setter must handle any pass.

Drill: Passer throws 15 "bad" passes — varying locations, heights, distances. Setter must set Zone 4 high ball regardless. Hitter evaluates: "Hittable" or "Not hittable." Track hittable percentage.

Coaching Focus: Composure. Setters who panic on bad passes will compound the error. The goal is a high ball to Zone 4 on every bad pass — not a perfect set, just a hittable one. Composure is the primary skill being trained here.

Live
5-Set Progression Drill
Full team10 minFull court

Setup: 6v6 scrimmage setup. Setter must run a specific set sequence: Set 1 = Zone 4 outside, Set 2 = Zone 2 back, Set 3 = Zone 4, Set 4 = middle (if available), Set 5 = setter's choice.

Drill: Serve and receive normally. Setter follows the sequence regardless of blockers. After 5 sets, switch to free choice. Compare effectiveness: does sequenced setting or free-choice setting produce more kills?

Coaching Focus: Setters learn that unpredictability (varying location) creates better offensive outcomes than always going to their favorite set. The data from this drill often surprises young setters.

Quick Sets
Middle Quick Timing Drill
Setter + MB8 minFull court

Setup: Setter at Zone 3, middle blocker approaching from Zone 3 starting position. Tosser feeds balls to setter.

Drill: Middle calls out her approach timing (1st step, 2nd step, 3rd step) so setter can hear when to release the quick set. Setter and middle work together to find the timing where the set arrives as the middle is at the peak of her jump. 20 reps.

Coaching Focus: The quick set is a partnership between setter and hitter. The setter adjusts their release to match the hitter's timing — not the other way around. Each setter-middle pair develops their own timing through repetition.

Setter Dump
Dump Decision Drill
Setter + defense8 minFull court

Setup: 3 defenders on the opposite side in Zone 1, Zone 6, Zone 5 positions (leaving Zone 2 or Zone 3 open). Setter at the net.

Drill: Tosser feeds the ball to the setter. Setter must decide: dump now (Zones 2/3 are open) or set to a hitter (Zones 2/3 are covered). Coach randomly adjusts the defender positions before each rep. Setter calls "dump" or "set" out loud before contact. 15 reps.

Coaching Focus: Reading open floor space. The dump should be used when it's the best option — not randomly. Setters who dump when the defense is in position lose points; setters who dump when the floor is open steal points.

Chapter 7
Common Mistakes

Setting errors are among the most visible in volleyball — both to officials and to hitters. Here are the most common technical and tactical mistakes at the youth level and how to fix them before they become habits.

Mistake
Double Contact — Hands Not Simultaneous
The Fix: One hand arrives before the other at contact. Run the ceiling set drill daily — lying on the back, setting straight up. The ceiling set exposes any asymmetry in hand arrival because there's no movement to mask it. Also check hand shape: uneven hands (one more open than the other) cause uneven arrival. Make symmetry the obsession before worrying about direction.
Mistake
Setting Too Tight — Ball at the Tape
The Fix: Setter is contacting the ball too far forward (in front of their head rather than above) and releasing flat rather than upward. Place a cone 3 feet off the net at Zone 4 as the landing target. The set should arc — not travel flat. Have the setter visualize a rainbow arc from their hands to the Zone 4 antenna, not a line drive.
Mistake
Always Setting Zone 4 — No Variety
The Fix: Young setters default to Zone 4 on every set once they find one set that works. This makes the offense predictable — blockers cheat Zone 4 and stuff the attack. Once Zone 4 is consistent, require the setter to set Zone 2 (back) at least twice per scrimmage set. Track it: if fewer than 25% of sets go to Zone 2 or the middle in practice, the setter doesn't have a balanced offense yet.
Mistake
Telegraphing the Set Direction
The Fix: Setter turns their shoulders or looks directly at the target before releasing the ball. Run a blindfold drill: blocker on the opposite side calls out where they think the set is going before the ball is released. If the blocker is right more than 70% of the time, the setter is telegraphing. Practice keeping hands symmetrical above the head and delaying the shoulder rotation until the last split second.
Mistake
Panicking on Bad Passes — Setting Errors Out of System
The Fix: Under pressure, young setters rush to set a bad pass and create an error. Teach the rule: "If it's bad, set it high to Zone 4." Any pass that puts the setter off balance should result in a high outside set, no exceptions. Run the bad-pass setting drill where the tosser deliberately creates poor passes — force the setter to practice calm, consistent decision-making on imperfect balls.
Mistake
Not Moving to the Ball — Setting From Off-Balance
The Fix: Setter doesn't move feet before reaching for a ball slightly off target — instead they reach with their hands. Every reach produces an off-balance set. Run the wall-set footwork drill daily — ball rebounds slightly off target, setter must move feet to center it before setting. The rule: if the ball is not directly above your head, move your feet. Never reach.
Chapter 8 · Coach Only
4-Week Setting
Development Plan

Setters improve faster with daily repetition than any other position. Even 10 minutes of solo work (ceiling sets, wall sets) before practice produces dramatic improvement over 4 weeks. Prioritize setter development — your whole offense depends on it.

Week 1
Hand Shape
  • Drill: Ceiling sets — 3 sets of 20 every practice
  • Drill: Wall sets — footwork approach, 8 min
  • Focus: simultaneous contact, soft hands, wrist finish
  • No live setting this week — solo/partner only
  • Goal: 0 double-contact calls in partner setting
Week 2
Zone 4 Consistency
  • Drill: Target zone setting with cone at Zone 4
  • Introduce Zone 2 back set (secondary)
  • Focus: right foot forward, body facing Zone 4
  • Penetration timing in 6-2 if applicable
  • Goal: 70% of Zone 4 sets within 3 feet of cone
Week 3
Decisions + Out of System
  • Drill: Read & call drill — blocker reading
  • Drill: Bad pass setting (15 bad passes, set Zone 4)
  • Introduce the quick set for ready setters
  • Focus: composure, high ball on poor passes
  • Goal: Correct set decision 65% of the time in drill
Week 4
Live Integration
  • Drill: 5-set progression then free choice scrimmage
  • Drill: Dump decision drill — reading open court
  • Track Zone 2 vs Zone 4 set distribution in scrimmage
  • Focus: disguise, variety, composure under match pressure
  • Goal: 25%+ sets to Zone 2 or middle, 0 setter panics
Daily Setter Work
Setters should arrive 10 minutes early for solo work every practice: 50 ceiling sets, 30 wall sets, and 20 partner sets before team practice starts. Over a 16-week season, this adds 6,400 extra setting repetitions per setter. That's the difference between a setter who can run an offense and one who is still finding consistency mid-season.
