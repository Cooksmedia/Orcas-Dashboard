Practice On Your Own

At-Home Drills

No gym, no team, no problem. These drills need a ball, a wall, and 10-15 minutes. Doing even 2-3 of these a few times a week adds up fast.

🧱
Wall Serve Practice
Serving 5-10 min
Stand 15-20 feet from a wall. Practice your toss and contact motion — toss the ball, let it drop, then serve it into the wall using your serving motion. Focus on a consistent toss height every time before adding power.
🙌
Platform Wall Taps
Passing 10 min
Stand facing a wall, arms in passing platform. Toss the ball softly off the wall to yourself and pass it back — focus on keeping your platform flat and angled, not swinging your arms. Try for 20 in a row without dropping.
🎯
Toss & Catch — Setting Hands
Setting 10 min
Toss the ball up above your forehead and catch it in setting hand shape (triangle with thumbs and pointer fingers). Catch it with bent elbows, absorb, then push it back up. Builds hand shape memory without a partner.
🪜
Approach Footwork — No Ball
Footwork 5-10 min
Practice your 3-step hitting approach (left-right-left for righties) in slow motion, then full speed, no ball needed. Use a hallway or driveway. Focus on the big last step and arm swing-back timing.
⬆️
Jump Training — Box/Step Jumps
Vertical 10-15 min
Use a sturdy step or low box. 3 sets of 8-10 jumps — explode up using both arms to drive overhead. Land soft with bent knees. This builds the explosive power used in attacking and blocking.
🔁
Self-Toss Pancake/Dig Reps
Defense 10 min
Toss the ball a few feet to either side of you on a soft surface (grass, carpet, mat). Practice moving your feet first, getting low, and getting your platform under the ball before it drops.
💪
Core & Arm Conditioning
Strength 10 min
3 sets each: plank holds (30 sec), push-ups (8-12), and superman holds (20 sec) for shoulder/back strength used in serving and hitting. Builds the foundation for a powerful arm swing.
🎥
Mirror Form Check
Technique 5 min
In front of a mirror or by recording yourself on a phone, run through your serve motion, passing platform, and setting hand shape in slow motion. Compare to the videos above — small fixes add up.
Know Your Role

Position Focus Guides

Every position has different priorities. Here's what to focus on extra during your own practice time based on where you play.

🏐 Outside Hitter (OH)
  • Approach footwork & timing
  • Serve receive passing
  • Arm swing power & shoulder strength
  • Reading the block
🛡️ Middle Blocker (MB)
  • Block footwork (step-close)
  • Quick approach timing
  • Vertical jump training
  • Reading the setter's hands
🎯 Setter (S)
  • Hand shape consistency
  • Footwork to the ball
  • Setting both directions equally
  • Quick decision-making reps
🙌 Libero / Defensive Specialist
  • Passing platform consistency
  • Low body positioning & movement
  • Reading hitters early
  • Communication & court awareness

Speak The Language

Volleyball IQ — Attack Call Glossary

Every attack has a name and a timing — coaches, setters, and hitters all need to know these calls to communicate before and during a rally. Different teams sometimes use different names, but these are the most common calls you'll hear.

📄
Attack Names Reference Sheet
Labeled diagram — print & bring to practice
↓
📝
Blank Version
Fill in your own team's calls
↓
GO
2nd-step · FastLeft Side (OH)
A quick, in-system set to the outside hitter on the left. The hitter is already on the second step of her approach by the time the setter contacts the ball — one of the most common calls in the game.
HUT
1st-step · HighLeft Side (OH)
A higher, slower out-of-system set to the left-side hitter. The hitter is only on her first step (or hasn't started) when the ball is set — gives her time to adjust to an off-target pass.
RED
2nd-step · FastRight Side (Opp)
The right-side mirror of a "Go" — a quick, in-system set to the opposite hitter. Same second-step timing, just on the other side of the court.
5
1st-step · HighRight Side (Opp)
The right-side mirror of a "Hut" — a high, slower out-of-system set to the opposite hitter when the pass isn't perfect.
GAP / 31
3rd-stepMiddle
An in-system set to the middle attacker, landing between the left sideline and the middle of the court. The hitter is on her third step as the setter contacts the ball — a bit slower than a true quick.
1 / QUICK
3rd–4th StepMiddle
The classic quick attack — set to the middle hitter directly in front of the setter. Some teams run it on the 3rd step (slower), others on the 4th (faster). Often just called "Quick."
BACK-1
3rd–4th StepMiddle (Behind Setter)
Same quick timing as a "1," but set to the middle attacker running directly behind the setter instead of in front.
2
2nd-step · HigherMiddle
A higher set to a middle-of-the-court attacker. Can go to the middle blocker, or to a pin hitter running a combination play with the middle.
SLIDE
2nd–3rd StepRight Side (Middle)
A back set to a middle attacker who takes off on one foot along the right side of the net. The exact timing shifts slightly depending on where the setter contacts the ball.
BIC
2nd–3rd StepBack Row · Middle
An in-system back-row attack through the middle of the court — gives teams another scoring option beyond the front row.
D
1st–2nd StepBack Row · Right Side
A back-row attack on the right side. An in-system D is set on the 2nd step (faster); an out-of-system D is set on the 1st step (slower, higher).
SETTER DUMP
2nd ContactSetter Attack
Instead of setting the ball to a hitter, the front-row setter attacks it herself with one hand on the second contact — catching the defense off guard.
SET-OVER
2nd ContactSetter Attack
The setter sends the 2nd contact straight over the net (instead of setting a hitter), aiming for an open spot on the opponent's court to score directly.
🎮 Quick Game

True or False? Test Your Rules Knowledge

Tap a card to flip it and see if you got it right. Some of these trip up even experienced players!

A serve that touches the net and goes over is still in play.
Tap to flip
✅ TRUE
Unlike old-school rules, a serve that clips the net and lands in bounds is completely legal — keep playing!
A team gets 3 touches max before sending the ball back over the net.
Tap to flip
✅ TRUE
3 touches per side — usually pass, set, hit. A block doesn't count as one of your 3 touches!
The same player can hit the ball twice in a row (two separate touches).
Tap to flip
❌ FALSE
One player can't touch the ball twice in a row — except right after a block, which doesn't count as a touch for that player.
Back-row players are allowed to spike the ball from anywhere on the court.
Tap to flip
❌ FALSE
Back-row players can only attack from behind the 10-foot line (the attack line). Jump from in front of it and it's a fault!
Games are played to 25 points, win by 2.
Tap to flip
✅ TRUE
Sets go to 25, win by 2 (so 26-24, 27-25, etc.) — except a deciding 5th set, which only goes to 15.
You only score a point when your team is serving.
Tap to flip
❌ FALSE
Every rally is a point for someone — this is called "rally scoring." Win the rally, win the point, whether you served or not.
A ball that lands on the boundary line is OUT.
Tap to flip
❌ FALSE
If any part of the ball touches the line, it's IN. The line is part of the court — just like in tennis!
The Libero can serve.
Tap to flip
✅ TRUE
In most leagues, the Libero can serve — usually in one rotation spot. (Check your league's specific rule, since this varies!)
⚡ Quick Reference — Know This Before Game Day
🏐
Rotation
Every time your team wins back the serve, everyone rotates one spot clockwise.
🙌
3 Touches
Pass, set, hit — that's your team's max before the ball must cross the net.
🚫
No Catching
The ball must be hit cleanly, not caught, lifted, or thrown.
📏
Attack Line
Back-row players must jump from behind this line to attack — or it's a fault.
🔁
Side Out
If the receiving team wins the rally, they get the point AND the serve.
🏆
Match Format
Best of 5 sets — first to 25 (15 for set 5), win by 2.
