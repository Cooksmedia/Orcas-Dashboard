Chapter 1
Building Attackers

Attacking is what players think about when they first fall in love with volleyball. Developing hitters who can swing confidently, read the defense, and convert in-system and out-of-system sets transforms a passive offense into a match-winning machine.

3
Every offensive sequence in volleyball is designed to produce a 3-contact attack: pass, set, hit. The attacker's job is to make sure that third contact is a weapon — not just a prayer over the net.
Approach First, Arm Swing Second
Most coaches teach arm swing before the approach is consistent. This is backwards. A player with a perfect 4-step approach and a mediocre arm swing will outscore a player with a powerful arm swing and no approach — because the approach puts the hitter in the right place at the right time. Approach first, always.
The Attacker's Mindset
Young hitters are often afraid to swing hard because they're afraid to make errors. This mindset produces soft, easily dug attacks. Build an aggressive mindset: swing at every ball, aim for a spot, commit to the swing. A hitter who swings confidently and sometimes hits out has more upside than a hitter who pushes the ball over every time.
What This Guide Covers
  • Approach: The 4-step footwork pattern, timing, and how the approach generates power
  • Arm Swing: Loading, high elbow, snap-through, and follow-through
  • Contact & Angles: Where to hit, how to angle the ball, tipping vs. swinging
  • Reading the Block: Line vs. cross, tooling the block, and seam attacks
  • Drills & Plan: 8 drills from shadow swing to live blocking
Safety Note — Young Hitters
Arm swing mechanics must be taught before adding full power. Young players (7th–9th grade) are still developing shoulder stability. Never ask players to swing at full power before the mechanics are established — a shoulder injury at 13 can affect a player for years. Build mechanics at 60-70% power, then add speed over weeks.
Chapter 2
The 4-Step Approach

The approach converts a stationary starting position into explosive upward momentum at the net. Every step matters — timing, angle, and the final plant all determine how high the hitter gets and where they contact the ball.

Step 1
The First Step (Direction)
Right-handed hitters: left foot first (small step, setting direction). This first step sets the angle of the entire approach. Start behind the setter, angled toward Zone 4 or the expected set location. The first step is calm — no rushing, just establishing direction.
Cues
"Step toward where you want to end up"
"First step is direction, not speed"
Steps 2–3
Build Speed (Right-Left)
Right-left strides accelerate the approach. Step 2 (right) is medium speed. Step 3 (left) plants hard — this is where the body begins to convert horizontal momentum to vertical. Arms begin to swing back on Step 3, loading for the jump.
Cues
"Accelerate into the last two steps"
"Arms swing back on step 3"
Step 4
The Plant (Right — Brake)
The right foot plants hard in front of — and slightly to the right of — the left foot. Both feet end up side by side, parallel to the net. This "brake" step stops forward momentum and redirects it upward. Arms are fully loaded back behind the body at this point.
Cues
"Stomp the right foot to brake and push up"
"Feet finish parallel to the net"
"Bend knees on the plant — load the jump"
The Jump
Arm Swing to Vertical
Both arms swing forward and up explosively from the loaded-back position. The upward arm swing drives the jump — a hitter who doesn't use their arms loses 30-40% of jump height. Leave the ground off both feet, reaching the ball at the peak of the jump.
Cues
"Throw your arms at the ceiling to jump higher"
"Jump off both feet — not one"
"Reach the peak — don't swing early"
Timing
Reading the Set
The approach starts based on the set trajectory — not when the setter contacts the ball. A hitter who waits until the ball leaves the setter's hands is always late. Start the approach as the pass arrives to the setter, so the plant happens as the set reaches its peak.
Cues
"Start moving when the pass goes to the setter"
"Arrive early — wait at the net is better than rushing"
Angle
Approach Angle
Outside hitters approach from outside the court at a 45-degree angle toward the set. Never approach straight toward the net — this eliminates the cross-court angle. The angled approach keeps both line and cross-court options open until the last second.
Cues
"Approach from behind and outside — not straight"
"Your path curves to the net, not runs at it"
Chapter 3
Arm Swing Mechanics

The arm swing is the engine of the attack. Power comes from the sequence — load, elbow, snap — not from muscling the ball. A proper arm swing at 70% effort generates more power than a wild swing at 100% effort.

The Loading Phase
As the hitter jumps, the hitting arm swings back and up — elbow high, forearm dropping behind the head like a throwing motion. This "bow-and-arrow" loading position stores energy. The non-hitting arm also reaches up — this helps with balance and rotation. A player who doesn't load loses 40% of potential power.
High Elbow
The elbow of the hitting arm should be at ear height or above before the swing begins. Elbow high = power + angle control. Elbow low = arm push with no snap, ball goes flat and short. Watch every hitter from the side: if the elbow is below the shoulder at the start of the swing, that's the fix.
The Snap-Through
The swing accelerates from the loaded position through contact — elbow leads, forearm follows, wrist snaps at contact. The snap at the wrist generates the last 20-30% of power and controls the ball's direction. Wrist snap over the top = topspin (ball dives down). Wrist snap flat = straight drive. Players who push the ball instead of snapping have no wrist involvement.
Follow-Through
The arm continues across the body after contact — the hitting arm finishes near the opposite hip. A full follow-through indicates a complete swing. Players who stop their arm at contact are decelerating before contact — robbing themselves of power. The follow-through is a result of a complete swing, not something to think about separately.
Contact Point — Where and When
Contact should happen at full arm extension above the hitting shoulder — the highest point the arm can reach at the peak of the jump. Contact too far forward = hitting into the net. Contact too far behind = ball goes out of bounds. Contact at the right point with a wrist snap produces a ball that drives down into the court.

The contact surface is the heel of the palm — the hard base of the hand — not the fingers. Full-palm contact at full extension with a wrist snap is the single most important mechanical combination in attacking.
Chapter 4
Contact & Hitting Angles

Where you aim matters as much as how hard you hit. A powerful swing at a defender is a free dig. A moderate swing at an open floor is a kill. Teach hitters to see the court — not just the ball.

The 3 Hitting Options
Every time a hitter contacts the ball, they have three choices:

1. Line: Ball hit down the sideline on the same side as the hitter. Shorter distance over the net, must thread past the line blocker. Effective when the line blocker is cheating cross-court.

2. Cross-Court (Angle): Ball hit diagonally across to the opposite corner. The most common and most reliable attack — longest distance over the net (hardest for defenders to cover) and widest court area to aim at.

3. Cut / Sharp Angle: Ball hit sharply to the near corner — a very tight cross-court. Risky but unreadable. Only use when the cross-court defender is deep.
Reading Open Floor
Before every approach, a hitter should take one glance at the defense to see where the open floor is. This takes 0.2 seconds and should become automatic. Where is the libero? Where is the right-back defender? A hitter who sees an uncovered corner before jumping can aim there deliberately instead of swinging blind.
Hitting Percentage vs. Power
At 7th–9th grade, a controlled swing aimed at open court almost always outperforms maximum-power hitting at a defender. A ball hit at 75% effort to the right spot is a better attack than a ball hit at 100% effort at the wrong spot. Teach hitters to choose a target, then swing at it — not to swing as hard as possible and hope.
Tipping
A tip (soft touch over or around the block) is a legitimate attack option — not a cop-out. The best time to tip: the block has two hands up and open floor exists behind it (Zone 2 or Zone 3), the set is tight to the net and a full swing risks a net touch, or the hitter is off-balance and needs to get the ball over safely.
Bad tips (pushed over the middle of the court to a waiting libero) are not strategy — they're panic. Only tip with a specific target.
Chapter 5
Reading the Block

A hitter who can read the block and adjust in real time is almost impossible to stop consistently. Block-reading is an advanced skill — but introducing the concepts early gives players a framework to develop over the season.

The Block Read
Teach hitters to look at the block — not away from it — at the peak of their jump. What to look for in 0.2 seconds: Are both hands up? Is there a seam? Is the outside hand angled line or cross? A single glance at the block arms tells the hitter what's open on the other side.
Hitting the Seam
A seam is a gap between the two blockers' hands. When the middle and outside blockers don't close completely, a hitter can drive the ball through the seam for a kill that neither blocker can reach. Seam attacks are the highest-percentage attack against a two-person block — harder to defend than a line or cross-court shot, and doesn't require the hitter to go around the block.
Tooling the Block
"Tooling" the block means deliberately hitting the ball into the blocker's hands so it deflects out of bounds. When to tool: the blockers have a solid wall with no seam and the set is outside enough to access the outside blocker's hand. Aim for the outside edge of the blocker's outside hand — the ball deflects off at an angle and goes out of bounds. This is a smart, intentional attack — not an error.
Roll Shot & Wipe
A roll shot is a soft, arcing attack that drops behind the block — slower than a full swing but uses the same approach and arm motion. Use the roll shot when the back-row defense is deep (Z1, Z5, Z6 all deep) and the zone directly behind the block is open. The "wipe" is the same concept off the blocker's hands — hit the ball off the blocker's hands so it drops on your side for a re-play or deflects out.
Building Read Habits
Run blocking-read drills where a coach or player holds a "shield" (two hands in the air) as the hitter approaches. As the hitter jumps, the coach moves the shield left or right — the hitter must hit away from the shield. This trains the eyes-up habit before live blocking pressure is added. Players who practice reading the shield become natural block-readers in match situations.
Chapter 7
Common Mistakes

Hitting errors at the youth level almost always trace back to approach, arm swing, or contact point. Here are the most common ones and exactly how to fix them.

Mistake
Hitting Into the Net — Contact Too Far Forward
The Fix: The ball is being contacted too far in front of the body — the arm is fully forward at the moment of contact, driving the ball down and into the net. Fix: have the player toss the ball slightly behind their hitting shoulder (not in front) and make contact directly above the hitting shoulder. The ball should arc UP slightly before coming down. Shadow approach drill: freeze at the top, check contact point position before adding the ball.
Mistake
Approaching Too Straight — No Angle Options
The Fix: Player runs directly at the net instead of curving in from outside. This eliminates the cross-court angle because the hitter's body is already facing line. Run the shadow approach drill with a cone at Zone 4 — the hitter must approach from outside and behind the cone, curving in. The approach path should be a curve (outside-in), not a straight line.
Mistake
No Arm Load — Pushing the Ball Instead of Swinging
The Fix: Player's hitting arm stays in front of the body and pushes the ball forward. There is no load (arm going back behind the head) and therefore no power. Run standing arm swing wall drill — make the player exaggerate the load phase: elbow goes back behind the ear before every swing. If they can't get the ball to hit the wall hard from 10 feet, they're not loading. Force the load in every drill until it becomes automatic.
Mistake
Late Timing — Swinging After the Peak
The Fix: Player approaches too late and contacts the ball on the way down from the set peak — producing a flat, downward angle that goes out. Fix the approach timing: hitter should start moving as the pass goes to the setter, not after the setter contacts the ball. Run approach timing drills: coach holds the ball, hitter starts the approach, coach releases on Step 2 of the approach. Hitter adjusts timing from there.
Mistake
Always Hitting the Same Spot
The Fix: Hitter only hits one direction — usually cross-court — making them completely predictable. Run the 3-cone targeting drill twice per practice until the player can deliberately aim all three zones. Make it competitive: a hitter who can only hit cross-court scores 0 for any cross-court hit in the targeting drill — they must demonstrate line and seam attacks before cross-court counts again.
Mistake
Eyes Down at the Peak — Can't Read the Block
The Fix: At the peak of the jump, the player looks down at the ball instead of up at the block. Run the shield read drill — this is exactly what fixes this issue. The player cannot complete the drill correctly without looking up. Make the shield drill a weekly staple. After 3–4 weeks, ask players what the blocker's hands were doing after every live attack — if they can answer, they're developing the eyes-up habit.
