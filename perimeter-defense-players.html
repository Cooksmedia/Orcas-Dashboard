Chapter 1
What Is Perimeter Defense

The Perimeter Defense is the Orcas' base defensive system. Understanding why we run it — and what it's designed to stop — gives every player the context to execute it with intention, not just position.

2-0-4
Two blockers at the net. Zero players behind the block. Four defenders spread along the deep court perimeter — protecting the lines, corners, and sidelines where hard-driven attacks land.
The Core Philosophy
Perimeter defense — also called "man-back" or "6-back" defense — protects the deep court first. The philosophy: force the opponent to hit a perfect soft shot to score. We take away the hard swing, the line shot, and the deep cross. If they want to beat the Orcas, they have to tip perfectly over a block into a tight space. That's a low-percentage play.
Why This System for Orcas
  • Easy to learn: four back defenders stay deep and read — no complex rotations to memorize at first
  • Stops power: at 7th–9th grade, most attacks are hard-driven. We cover what kills us most
  • Builds reading: deep position gives defenders time to read the hitter's shoulder before moving
  • Strong with a good block: our blockers take away angles, defenders only cover what's left
Strengths
  • Excellent coverage of hard-driven deep attacks to all four corners
  • Line coverage is automatic — Zone 1 always owns the line
  • Simple starting positions — players learn their base quickly
  • Works regardless of blocker effectiveness — even one blocker helps
  • Defenders face inward = they see court and read hitters clearly
Known Weakness: The Tip
The perimeter leaves the center of the court open. Tips into Zone 3 (middle front) and sharp roll shots behind the block are the vulnerability. We address this with our front-row non-blockers, Zone 6 forward reads, and a tip-alert adjustment (covered in Attack Scenarios). Understanding the weakness is part of running the system intelligently.
How It Fits Our Offense
Every successful dig in the perimeter defense leads to the same sequence: dig → Zone 3 setter → attack. The deep starting position of our defenders means digs go high and toward the setter naturally. Unlike rotation defense (where off-blockers come off the net), perimeter keeps our front row blockers at the net — ready to transition immediately to an offensive approach if the dig is clean. Defense feeds offense in this system.

The Starting Rule
All four back-row defenders start in their perimeter positions with one foot near the sideline or end line — facing inward and facing the hitter. This gives them a read angle on the ball and a clear view of the attack. Movement is always inward toward the ball — never backing up.
Adjust With the Set
Starting positions shift based on where the set goes:

Set left (Zone 4): Z1 drops deep to the line, Z5 slides inside for cross, Z6 middle-left

Set right (Zone 2): Z5 drops deep to the line, Z1 slides inside for cross, Z6 middle-right

Set middle: Z1 and Z5 hold, Z6 backs directly behind the block
The "Freeze" Rule — Most Important Concept
At the moment the hitter contacts the ball — STOP. No defender should be moving when the attack is struck. A player caught mid-step cannot change direction quickly enough to react to a ball hit away from them. The sequence is: read the serve → adjust position → freeze in a low athletic stance at hitter contact → explode toward the ball. Defenders who are still walking or shuffling when the hitter swings will miss balls they should get.
Chapter 3
Zone Responsibilities

Every player on the court has a specific zone and responsibility in the perimeter defense. Know your zone, own your zone. No one is a spectator — even the non-blocking front-row players have tip coverage assignments.

Zone 1 · Right Back
The Line Owner
Zone 1 is responsible for the line shot when the set goes left (Zone 4 outside hitter). Drop back to the deep right corner — 6-8 feet from the end line, near the sideline. Face the hitter. On a right-side attack, Zone 1 reads for the cross-court angle.
Primary Responsibilities
Deep line shot (left-side attack)
Cross-court angle (right-side attack)
Any ball to the right sideline or corner
Zone 5 · Left Back (Libero)
The Cross-Court Digger
Zone 5 — usually the Libero — covers the deep cross-court shot from a right-side or outside attack. Slide to the inside of the blocker's hands, 6-8 feet from the end line. On a right-side attack, Zone 5 drops to the line. The Libero's reading ability makes them most valuable in this deep zone.
Primary Responsibilities
Deep cross-court (left-side attack)
Line shot (right-side attack)
Sharp angle attack from Zone 2
Zone 6 · Middle Back
The Safety Valve
Zone 6 backs to the end line directly behind the block. Covers shots that split Z1 and Z5 — middle deep, seam balls, and anything that passes through the block. Zone 6 is the last line of defense. Default rule: if no one else calls it, Zone 6 goes.
Primary Responsibilities
Middle deep shots through the block
Seam balls between Z1 and Z5
Any unclaimed ball deep center
Zone 4 · Left Front (Non-Blocker)
The Tip Guard
When the outside blocker (Zone 4 front) is NOT in the block, they drop off the net to cover tips in the front-left area. Position at the 10-foot line, facing the hitter. On a right-side attack, the Zone 4 player often joins the block — check your assignments each rally.
Primary Responsibilities
Tip coverage (left-front area)
Short roll shots to Zone 4/5 area
Transition to approach after dig
Zone 2 · Right Front (Non-Blocker)
Right-Side Tip Guard
Zone 2 non-blocker covers tips to the right-front area when the right-side player is not in the block. Position at the 10-foot line near Zone 2/3 seam. On a left-side attack, the Zone 2 player is typically the setter — they must cover the right tip zone after releasing the set.
Primary Responsibilities
Tip coverage (right-front area)
Short roll shots near Zone 2/3
Setter release after set → tip coverage
Communication
Every Touch Called
Every defender must call "mine!" before contacting every ball. Seam balls require the player moving toward the ball to call it. Zone 6 calls "help!" if a ball is going to another zone. Any ball touched without a call is a communication error — track these in practice as seriously as a missed dig.
Communication Standards
"Mine!" — called before every contact
"Help!" — Zone 6 signals ball going to another zone
"Tip!" — front-row player alerts to a short ball
"Line!" or "Cross!" — call read before hitter contacts
Chapter 4
Attack Scenarios

The perimeter defense looks different depending on where the set goes. Here's exactly where everyone should be for each of the three primary attack scenarios — and how to adjust for the tip.

Scenario 1: Left-Side Attack (Zone 4 Outside Hitter)
The most common attack at youth level — this is the primary assignment to know cold.

Blockers: Outside blocker (Z4) + Middle blocker (Z3) form a double block at the left pin.

Zone 1 (Right Back): Drop deep to the right corner — 6-8 feet from the end line, near the right sideline. Own the line. Face the hitter.

Zone 5 (Left Back / Libero): Slide to the inside of the left blocker's outside hand — dig the deep cross-court angle.

Zone 6 (Middle Back): Back up to the end line, slightly left of center — cover the ball that splits Z1 and Z5.

Zone 2 (Right Front / non-blocker): Off the net at the 10-foot line — cover tips to right front.

Tip adjustment: If Zone 5 reads "tip" (hitter's elbow drops, arm slows), Zone 5 runs in toward the 10-foot line. Zone 6 fills left-back deep. Zone 1 holds line.
Scenario 2: Right-Side Attack (Zone 2)
Blockers: Right-side blocker (Z2) + Middle blocker (Z3) block the right pin.

Zone 5 (Left Back): Drops deep to the left sideline — now Zone 5 owns the LINE (mirrored from Scenario 1).

Zone 1 (Right Back): Slides inside toward center — covers the deep cross-court angle from Zone 2.

Zone 6: Backs up, slightly right of center.

Zone 4 (Left Front): Off the net — covers tips to left front.

Key: Zone 1 and Zone 5 FLIP their responsibilities — this is the most common confusion. Remind players: "Whoever is on the side of the attack owns the line."
Scenario 3: Middle Attack (Zone 3)
Blockers: Middle blocker (Z3) + one wing blocker form a double block at Zone 3.

Zone 1 and Zone 5: Both hold wide — split the court evenly, deep sideline positions. The middle attack goes either line-left or line-right; Z1 and Z5 each own their side.

Zone 6: Directly behind the block at the end line — covers any ball that gets through or tips over.

Non-blockers: Both cover tips at the 10-foot line, one left, one right of the block.

Read key: Middle attacks are fast — Zone 6 must be in position BEFORE the quick set, not after it's called.
The Tip Adjustment — Adding to the Perimeter
The perimeter's weakness is tips into the center. Our fix: read-based tip adjustment without changing the base system.

When to run the tip adjustment: hitter's approach is slow or off-balance, set is tight to the net, hitter's elbow drops during the arm swing, or the score situation calls for taking away the easy tip.

The adjustment: The front-row player nearest the tip zone calls "TIP!" and steps up to the 10-foot line. Zone 6 fills the deep center. No other players move — the perimeter stays intact.

What NOT to do: Don't send Zone 6 forward on every play — this leaves the deep center open and turns a perimeter into something in between two systems. Commit to one adjustment or the other.
Chapter 5
Reading & Adjusting

The perimeter defense is not a static formation. Defenders who read the hitter and block actively outperform those who simply stand in their zone. Here's how to train an active, reading-based perimeter.

Read 1
Read the Set First
Before the hitter contacts the ball, the set's position tells you what attacks are possible. Tight set = tip or sharp angle likely. High outside set = full range of attacks. Low set = flat, hard cross likely, less power available. Read the set to pre-position yourself — the best defenders start moving on the set flight, not the swing.
Cues
"Tight set = step forward, not back"
"High set = back up, cover deep first"
Read 2
Read the Hitter's Shoulder
The hitter's hitting shoulder at the peak of the jump predicts shot direction 70% of the time. Shoulder square to net = cross-court. Shoulder turned to the line = line shot. Defenders in Z1 and Z5 should call "line!" or "cross!" based on this read — out loud, before contact.
Cues
"Shoulders tell you first"
"Call it before they hit"
Read 3
Read the Block
Your blockers are your first line — watch what they're taking away. If the block seals the line, Z1 can shade toward the seam. If the block is split (gap between hands), Z6 must move behind the gap. Defense and block work together — communicate.
Cues
"If the block seals line — shade seam"
"Gap in the block — Z6 fills it"
Read 4
The Freeze Point
Stop moving when the hitter contacts the ball. A defender mid-step at contact is delayed by 0.3–0.5 seconds — enough for a hard ball to beat them. Read, move, freeze, react. The freeze is the most trainable defensive habit and the most ignored.
Cues
"Low and still when they hit"
"Contact = freeze, then explode"
Read 5
Hitter Tendencies
In a match, defenders should actively track what each hitter prefers: Does the #4 always go line? Does their middle always tip? After 3–4 attacks, defenders should adjust their starting position based on this data. The coach communicates tendencies between sets; defenders apply them in the next set.
Cues
"What's she done twice? Expect it again"
"Cheat one step toward her favorite spot"

Chapter 8
Common Mistakes

These are the most common perimeter defense errors at the 7th–9th grade level — and the specific corrections that fix each one.

Mistake
Starting Too Close to the Net
The Fix: Players drifting toward the net during rally — not holding their perimeter. Remind: "Deep first, always deep." Mark the perimeter starting positions with tape on the court and enforce them during walk-through drills. Hard balls land deep — if you're too close to the net, you'll never reach them. Run the set-and-freeze drill until deep starting positions are automatic.
Mistake
Zone 1 and Zone 5 Not Swapping on Right-Side Attack
The Fix: The most common error in the perimeter — Z1 and Z5 forget to flip their line/cross responsibilities when the attack switches sides. Run ghost defense explicitly for right-side attack 3x more than left-side attack until the swap is automatic. Verbal cue: "Whoever is on the attack side owns the LINE." Post this on the bench. Quiz players during practice.
Mistake
Still Moving When the Hitter Contacts the Ball
The Fix: The freeze rule is the most violated rule in the perimeter. Players who are mid-step when the attack is struck can't change direction quickly enough. Run the set-and-freeze drill and stop it the moment any player is caught moving at "hit." Make it a team standard: moving at contact = sit out one rep. After two weeks of enforcement, the freeze becomes automatic.
Mistake
Zone 6 Pulling Forward Without a Read
The Fix: Zone 6 is not a tip guard in the base perimeter — that's the front-row non-blocker's job. A Zone 6 who creeps forward every rally leaves the deep center wide open. Remind: "Zone 6 stays deep until you READ a tip — not just guess." The tip-alert system relies on the front-row player calling it first. Zone 6 responds, not leads.
Mistake
No Verbal Calling — Silent Defense
The Fix: Run the calling drill — any touch without a verbal "mine" is dead ball, point to opponent. Silence in perimeter defense is a breakdown waiting to happen. Seam balls between Z1 and Z5, or Z5 and Z6, require calling to avoid both players watching the ball drop between them. Enforce calling in every drill for two consecutive weeks — it becomes a habit.
Mistake
Digging Without Direction — Ball Goes Anywhere
The Fix: Players dig the ball "up" but with no Zone 3 target. Run the coach-hit perimeter drill and track Zone 3 reach percentage separately from dig percentage. Remind: "A dig that doesn't reach the setter is only 50% of the job." Add a setter at Zone 3 in practice to make the target physical and real — the setter jumping for every dig gives defenders immediate feedback on dig quality.
