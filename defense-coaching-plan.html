Chapter 1 · Foundation
Base Defense

The Base Defense is where every player starts on every rally — before the set is made, before the attack direction is known. Getting every player to their correct base position consistently is the single most important defensive skill to train first.

The 2-0-4 System
2 blockers at the net. 0 players behind the block. 4 defenders spread along the deep perimeter. The perimeter is your base — defenders start near the sidelines and end line, facing inward, ready to move toward the ball. This covers hard-driven attacks first. The block takes away angles; back-row defenders cover what the block allows through.
Three Defensive Phases
  1. Base Position: Where everyone starts before the opponent contacts the ball. Everyone holds base until the set direction is clear.
  2. Read Position: Defenders adjust based on where the set goes — left, right, or middle.
  3. React Phase: Freeze at hitter contact. Explode to the ball after contact.
The Freeze Rule
At the moment the hitter contacts the ball — every defender must be stopped. A player who is still moving at contact cannot change direction to reach a ball hit away from them. The sequence: read → adjust position → freeze in a low stance at contact → explode toward the ball. This is the most violated rule and the most important one.
The Tip Weakness
Perimeter defense leaves Zone 3 (middle front) open. The fix: the off-blocker (non-blocking front-row player) drops to the 10-foot line to cover tips. Zone 6 does NOT come forward — Zone 6 stays deep until a tip read is confirmed by a front-row player calling "TIP!"
Base Position — Each Player's Job
Blockers
MB + OH at Net
Two blockers start at the net in base. They are responsible for blocking, not covering floor until they land. After landing from a block attempt, both transition to their defensive assignments based on the attack scenario.
Zone 1 · Right Back
Deep Right Corner
8-10 feet from end line, near right sideline. Faces the hitter. In base, Z1 is the right-back anchor — deep enough to cover anything hit to the right corner. Does not cheat forward or toward center in base.
Cue
"Deep right, facing the setter — not the net"
Zone 5 · Left Back (Libero)
Deep Left Corner
8-10 feet from end line, near left sideline. The Libero's base position. In base, Z5 is the left-back anchor. Does not start inside the blockers' reach — stays wide. Adjusts inward based on set direction.
Cue
"Deep left — one foot near the line"
Zone 6 · Middle Back
Deep Center
At or near the end line, center of the court. Zone 6 is the safety valve — covers any ball that splits Z1 and Z5. Stays deep in base. Does NOT creep forward. If no one else calls it, Zone 6 takes it.
Cue
"End line center — last line of defense"
Off-Blocker · Front Row
Tip Coverage (10-ft line)
The non-blocking front-row player drops to the 10-foot line in base — not deep, not at the net. Their job is tips and short balls. Reads the hitter's elbow — elbow drops = tip incoming. Calls "TIP!" loudly to alert teammates.
Cue
"10-foot line — watch the elbow, not the ball"
Communication
Call Everything
Every touch needs a verbal "mine!" called before contact. Seam balls must be called. Off-blocker calls "TIP!" Zone 6 calls "Help!" when a ball is going to another zone. Silent defense = broken defense. No exceptions.
Cue
"If you don't call it, we don't count it"

The Golden Rule: Whoever Is on the Attack Side Owns the Line
When the set goes left (Zone 4), Zone 1 (right back) is on the OPPOSITE side of the attack — so Zone 1 owns the LINE (the right sideline is where the line shot goes from a Z4 hitter). Zone 5 slides inside to cover the cross-court angle. This is the adjustment that teams get wrong most often — make it a daily verbal quiz until it's automatic.
Zone 1 — Owns the Line
Drop deep to the right corner — 6-8 feet from the end line, 2-3 feet inside the right sideline. Face the outside hitter. The line shot from Zone 4 travels diagonally to this corner. Do not cheat toward center. The line is your ball. If the hitter's shoulder turns to the line — step toward it.
Cues
"Set goes left — I own the line"
"Deep right corner — don't come in until I read a tip"
Zone 5 / Libero — Cross-Court
Slide inside the outside blocker's outside hand — position yourself inside the blockers' reach so you cover the cross-court angle. The cross-court from Zone 4 is the highest-percentage attack — most hitters go angle. Face the hitter. Read the shoulder: square = cross, turned = line.
Cues
"Set goes left — I slide inside for cross-court"
"Inside the blocker's hands, deep left"
Zone 6 — Deep Center-Left
Zone 6 shifts slightly left of center for an outside attack. Stays at the end line. Covers: balls that split Z1 and Z5, balls that get through or over the block, and any seam ball between the two back-row defenders. Never comes forward unless Z5 calls "TIP!" and releases forward.
Cue
"Shift slightly left — end line — safety valve"
Off-Blocker (Z2) — Right-Front Tip
The non-blocking front-row player (typically Zone 2 / right front) drops off the net to the 10-foot line on the right side. Watches the hitter's elbow and arm speed. Elbow drops = tip. Calls "TIP!" loudly. Zone 6 does NOT move until this call is made.
Cue
"10-foot line right side — read the elbow first"
"Call TIP loud enough for Z6 to hear"
Reading the Outside Hitter
Three pre-contact reads for defenders:

1. Set height & location: High outside set = full options. Tight set = tip/sharp angle likely. Ball outside the antenna = only cross-court available (angle becomes impossible).

2. Approach angle: Straight approach = both line and cross open. Approach from deep outside = harder to hit line cleanly, expect cross.

3. Hitting shoulder at peak: Square to net = cross-court. Turned toward line = line shot. Call it before contact: "LINE!" or "CROSS!" out loud.

The Flip — Z1 and Z5 Swap When the Set Goes Right
When the set goes right (Zone 2), the roles of Z1 and Z5 completely reverse:

Zone 5 (Libero) now owns the LINE — drops to the deep left sideline, covers the line shot from Zone 2.
Zone 1 now covers CROSS-COURT — slides inside to cover the diagonal angle from Zone 2 to Zone 5 area.

This flip is the most common mistake in perimeter defense. Make "whoever is on the attack side owns the line" the verbal cue that triggers the flip automatically.
Zone 5 / Libero — Owns the Line
Drop deep to the left sideline — 6-8 feet from the end line, 2-3 feet inside the left sideline. Face the right-side hitter. The line shot from Zone 2 travels to the left back corner. This is now the Libero's line responsibility.
Cues
"Set goes right — I own the left line"
"Deep left corner — don't drift center"
Zone 1 — Cross-Court
Slide inside toward center — covers the cross-court angle from Zone 2. The cross-court from a right-side hitter goes toward Zone 5/6 area. Zone 1 must slide inside to intercept this angle. Zone 1 does NOT own the line when the set goes right.
Cues
"Set goes right — I slide inside for cross"
"Move toward the seam, not the corner"
Zone 6 — Deep Center-Right
Zone 6 shifts slightly right of center for a right-side attack. Stays at the end line. Same role as in the outside attack scenario — covers seam balls, balls through the block, and any ball no one else claims.
Cue
"Shift slightly right — end line — safety valve"
Off-Blocker (Z4) — Left-Front Tip
When the attack goes right, the Zone 4 player (left front) is the non-blocker. They drop to the 10-foot line on the LEFT side — covering tips that land near Zone 4. They read the right-side hitter's elbow and call "TIP!" to alert Z6.
Cue
"Set goes right — I'm at the 10-foot line left"
Reading the Right-Side Hitter
Right-side hitters are often left-handed — or right-handers who favor the sharp cross-court cut to Zone 5. Key reads:

Shoulder square: expect cross-court to Z5/Z6 area.
Shoulder turned left: line shot down the left sideline — Z5 must hold deep.
Tight set near the net: expect a tip over the block to Zone 4 — Z4 off-blocker must be ready.
High set far from net: expect a hard cross-court or sharp angle — Z1 slides further inside.
Chapter 4 · Implementation
4-Week Practice Plan

12 drills across 4 weeks — progressing from no-ball formation walks to full live-ball defensive systems with scoring. Dedicate 12–15 minutes per practice to defensive system work. Do not skip weeks — the foundation built in Week 1 makes everything in Week 4 possible.

Week 1
Base Positions
  • Ghost Defense Walk-Through — all 3 scenarios no ball
  • Set-and-Freeze drill — position before every "hit"
  • Quiz: Z1 and Z5 responsibilities by scenario (verbal)
  • No live attacking — positioning only
  • Goal: Every player finds correct position in all 3 scenarios
Week 2
Reading + Adjusting
  • Shoulder Read drill — no ball, call "line!" or "cross!"
  • Coach-Hit Perimeter — slow/medium pace, all 3 scenarios
  • Z1/Z5 Flip Drill — set goes left, set goes right (rapid fire)
  • Calling drill — no silent touches enforced
  • Goal: 70%+ correct shoulder reads, zero silent touches
Week 3
Tip Defense + Live Ball
  • Tip Alert Drill — off-blocker reads elbow, calls TIP
  • Full coach-hit at match pace — line, cross, tip, seam
  • 5-on-6 scrimmage: attacking side runs Z4 and Z2 attacks
  • Add dig direction tracking — count digs reaching Zone 3
  • Goal: Under 2 uncovered tips per 15 reps in scrimmage
Week 4
Match Integration
  • Full rally scrimmage with freeze-check scoring
  • Consecutive dig challenge — team record
  • Watch film — identify correct vs incorrect positions
  • Hitter tendency tracking: what does #12 prefer?
  • Goal: Correct position 80%+ of freeze checks
All 12 Drills
Week 1 · No Ball
Ghost Defense Walk-Through
Full team8 minFull court

Setup: Full team on their side of the court, in their starting rotation positions. Coach calls scenarios.

Drill: Coach calls "Zone 4 left!" / "Zone 2 right!" / "Middle!" Players walk to correct perimeter positions. Freeze and evaluate: Is Z1 deep right? Is Z5 inside for cross? Is Z6 at the end line? Is off-blocker at the 10-foot line? 3 rounds of each scenario, correct each position explicitly before moving on.

Focus: This is the most important early-season drill. Players who can't find their position without a ball will not find it during a live rally. No shortcuts.

Week 1 · Positioning
Set-and-Freeze
Full team10 minFull court

Setup: Coach on the opposite side with a ball. Players in base positions.

Drill: Coach tosses a high ball simulating a set — to Z4, Z2, or middle. Players must move to their adjusted positions AND FREEZE before the coach "hits" (catches the ball). Coach evaluates and corrects. 15 reps total, rotating through all three scenarios.

Focus: Are players moving during the set flight or after? Reinforce: move with the set, freeze before the hit.

Week 2 · Read Training
Shoulder Read — No Ball
Partners8 minAny space

Setup: Partner A simulates a hitting approach and turns their shoulder — line or cross — at the peak. Z1 and Z5 defenders in position read and call it.

Drill: Hitter swings without a ball. At the peak, turn shoulder to line or cross. Defenders call "LINE!" or "CROSS!" before the swing completes. Track: correct call % per player. Goal 75%+ by end of Week 2. Switch hitter every 10 reps.

Focus: When is the call made? After the swing = reaction, not a read. The call must happen DURING the swing based on shoulder angle.

Week 2 · Position Drill
Z1/Z5 Flip Drill (Rapid Fire)
3 defenders6 minFull court

Setup: Z1, Z5, and Z6 in base position. Coach calls set locations rapidly.

Drill: Coach calls "LEFT!" or "RIGHT!" every 3–4 seconds. Z1 and Z5 shuffle to their correct positions for that scenario and hold. Coach evaluates. Reset to base. Repeat 20 times in 6 minutes. No ball — pure positioning speed.

Focus: Automaticity. Z1 and Z5 should flip instantly without thinking. If there's hesitation, the player is still thinking through it rather than reacting. More reps until it's a reflex.

Week 2 · Live Ball
Coach-Hit Perimeter Drill
3–4 defenders12 minFull court

Setup: Z1, Z5, Z6 in defensive positions. Coach on a box above the net hitting down at the back court. Target (hoop or cone) at Zone 3.

Drill: Coach calls "Zone 4!" then hits line, cross, or seam — varying pace. Defenders dig to Zone 3 target. Track: (1) correct position before hit, (2) dig reaching Zone 3. 20 attacks per group.

Focus: Both things simultaneously — correct position AND dig direction. A good dig from a wrong position means they got lucky. Correct position + controlled dig = the system working.

Week 2 · Communication
No Silent Touches
Full team10 minFull court

Setup: 6v6 scrimmage with one rule added: any ball touched without a verbal "mine!" call is dead — point to the opponent.

Drill: Normal rally-score game. Every touch must be verbally called. Track uncalled touches per set. Goal: zero by end of Week 2. Run for two full sets.

Focus: Building calling as a non-negotiable habit. Immediate, consistent consequence makes the rule stick faster than any amount of reminding.

Week 3 · Tip Defense
Tip Alert Drill
4–6 players10 minFull court

Setup: Coach at Zone 4 or Zone 2 (simulating a hitter). Two blockers at net. Three back-row defenders in perimeter. Off-blocker at 10-foot line.

Drill: Coach randomly tips OR swings full — no telegraph. Off-blocker must read elbow and call "TIP!" before the ball lands. Back-row holds on a swing, Z6 stays deep. Track: tips covered vs landing untouched. Goal: under 2 uncovered per 15 reps.

Focus: Off-blocker's elbow read. If they're watching the back-row instead of the hitter, they'll always be late. Drill the read, not the movement.

Week 3 · Scenario Drill
Left-Right Scenario Switch
Full team12 minFull court

Setup: Attacking side with a setter and two hitters — one at Zone 4 (OH), one at Zone 2 (RS). Defending side runs full perimeter.

Drill: Setter alternates sets — outside then right-side in unpredictable order. Back-row defenders must flip their line/cross assignments based on where the set goes. Coach watches Z1 and Z5 exclusively — are they flipping correctly? 20 rallies, rotate hitters every 5.

Focus: The flip under real set-flight timing. Players who nail it in the Z1/Z5 Flip Drill but fail here need more live reps with the actual set trajectory as the trigger.

Week 3 · Full System
5-on-6 Perimeter Scrimmage
Full team15 minFull court

Setup: Attacking side of 5 (coach feeds sets). Defending side 6 players running full perimeter system.

Drill: Attacking side runs 3 attacks per rotation (Z4, Z2, middle) then rotates. Defensive point for each dig to Zone 3. Point lost for each ball that hits the floor in a covered zone. Track score, post it, compete. First team to 15 defensive points wins.

Focus: Communication + position + dig direction simultaneously. Add a freeze check mid-drill — pause before a hit and check all positions.

Week 4 · Pressure
20-Ball Consecutive Defense
Full team10 minFull court

Setup: Coach with 20 balls, hitting continuously at varying zones and scenarios. Full defensive team.

Drill: How many consecutive balls can the team dig before one hits the floor untouched? Any uncalled touch resets to zero. Track team record. Celebrate records. After a miss: "Who should have had that? What position would have gotten it?"

Focus: Urgency and the "nothing hits the floor" mindset. Players who give up on difficult balls stop the streak — this drill makes it personal.

Week 4 · Integration
Full Rally with Freeze-Check Scoring
Full team15 minFull court

Setup: Full 6v6 rally scoring. Coach tracks defensive positions.

Drill: Normal game, rally score. Before each attack, coach calls "FREEZE!" and evaluates all 4 defenders: +1 if all 4 are in correct perimeter positions for the current set scenario. 0 if anyone is out of place. Track both game score AND freeze-check score. Debrief after: which rotations had the best positioning?

Focus: Identifying which rotations break down under real pressure. Players who are great in drills but lose position in scrimmage are distracted by other aspects of the rally — identify what's pulling their attention and address it.

Week 4 · Film Study
Post-Match Film Review
Full team10 minClassroom/huddle

Setup: Game film from the Orcas YouTube channel. Pause at moments of defensive play.

Drill: Pause the film before each attack. Ask the team: "Is Z1 in the right position for this set? Is Z5 inside or outside? Is the off-blocker at the 10-foot line?" Then play through and see the result. 5–6 plays per session.

Focus: Connecting the practice positioning system to real match footage. Players who see themselves or their teammates in the wrong position on film retain the correction much longer than verbal coaching alone. Keep it positive — celebrate right positions as much as correcting wrong ones.

What to Track Weekly
Three numbers to post every week:

1. Freeze-check score: What % of freeze checks had all 4 defenders in correct perimeter positions? Target 80%+ by Week 4.

2. Dig-to-Zone-3 %: Of all digs, what % reached the setter area? Target 50%+ by Week 4.

3. Uncovered tips per set: Tips that land without a defender touching them. Target 0–1 per set by Week 4.

Post these on a whiteboard. Players who see their own data improve faster and hold each other accountable in ways coach correction alone cannot produce.
